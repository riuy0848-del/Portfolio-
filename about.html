About Vertex

Founded on a
simple load case.

In 1948, three engineers and a mathematician opened Vertex with one premise: theory only counts once it's been built, tested, and rebuilt better. That premise still runs the place.

Sheet 02 / Origins

A campus that treats every building as a working prototype

Vertex was chartered by a small group of postwar civil engineers who believed universities taught structures but rarely built them. The first class of 64 students spent as much time on the construction site as in lecture halls — a habit the university never dropped.

Today that founding instinct shows up in every department: economics students model real municipal budgets, computer science majors defend live systems in the campus red team lab, and civil engineering seniors close out their degree with a capstone built for an actual public client.

Sheet 03 / Timeline

Built in stages

Eight decades of additions, revisions, and the occasional total redesign.

1948

Vertex opens with three founding schools

Engineering, Mathematics, and Applied Sciences enroll a first class of 64 students.

1972

School of Computing established

One of the first standalone computing departments in the region, built around a single shared mainframe.

1998

Research funding crosses $40M

Vertex labs begin publishing in structural resilience, materials science, and early network security.

2019

Founders Hall renovation completed

The original 1951 structure is retrofitted using techniques developed by Vertex's own seismic lab.

2026

Applied Security Lab opens

A dedicated space inside the computing wing for network defense, cryptography, and CTF training.

Sheet 04 / Working Principles

What we optimize for

Three commitments that shape everything from admissions to curriculum design.

01

Build before you believe

Coursework favors working prototypes, tested models, and defensible calculations over untested theory.

02

Small ratios, real access

A 9:1 student-to-faculty ratio means undergraduates work directly with the people running the labs.

03

Rebuild what fails

Failed experiments are logged, published internally, and revisited — not buried at the end of a semester.
